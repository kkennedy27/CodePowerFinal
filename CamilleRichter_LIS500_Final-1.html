The Wisconsin Maritime Museum
The institution we will be addressing with our technology proposals is the Wisconsin
Maritime Museum in Manitowoc. The museum, which opened in 1969, hosts an impressive
collection of maritime artifacts and information on the history of the Great Lakes. Led by a
board of trustees, the museum is an independent heritage institution that receives funding
from both public interest initiatives, large private donations, and corporate partnerships.
Aside from its extensive physical collection, the Wisconsin Maritime Museum also hosts
its own database of digital reproductions and digitizations of various media from its
collection. Currently, the museum is in the process of digitizing over 30,000 handwritten
records of historical Great Lakes maritime data (Wisconsin Maritime Museum).
Group Proposal
Our proposals will be addressing potential technology adoptions and critical oversight on
the museums digital collections database. We will specifically be working with the Carus
Collection, a collection of photographs and ephemera from ship captain Edward Carus.
Our group-wide proposal is addressing potential technological tools and workflows that
would benefit or build on the current digital collection. Overall, we believe that while the
database is a fantastic resource given the limitations of the museum, there are some
simple, yet effective ways it can be improved using the tools we have acquired through this
class. Specifically, we as a group wish to target tools and workflows that may better assist
researchers and archivists in better understanding and utilizing the data within the online
collection. These tools can be used for a variety of purposes, such as research topic
generation, historical mapping, education, and data visualization.
Individual Proposal
In my proposal, I will utilizing Pandas and basic data analytics strategies to address the
issue of digital records, digitization, and its impact on cultural heritage institutions.
In place of example data from the Maritime Museum, I will be showcasing the data
analytics strategies using a collections dataset provided by the Museum of Modern Art
(MoMA). The substitute dataset is similar to the structure of any potential scraped data
from the Maritime Museums digital collection; categories in the repository include artifact
dimensions, webpage URLs, creator information and nationality, and artifact medium.
Having previously worked in GLAM institutions (Galleries, Libraries, Archives, and
Museums) I can attest that any internal collections repository at the Maritime Museum
would be organized in a very similar manner.
I am hoping for my proposal to ground the rest of my groups work by setting the stage for
further analytical work. Ultimately, the combined proposals should showcase the potential
of combining Python workflows with digital records and collections.
Argument: The application of data analytics on digital collections
Data analytics are a simple, yet effective way to showcase the power of digital records for
heritage sites. These techniques' broad purpose is to quickly and efficiently create
summaries and visualizations of large data repositories. A major challenge to any digitized
heritage collection such as the Carus collection is the removal of context. Critics of rapid
digitization fear that that when displayed digitally, artifacts or records are disconnected
from their original context, and will therefore lose meaning (Mallan & Park, 2006). I believe
that data analysis has the potential to give narrative to digital spaces, allowing users to find
meaning and power to a collection in a different way from the physical. When presented
hand-in-hand with digitized historical media, data visualizations allow both patrons from
the public and researchers to gain a comprehensive understanding of the collections
context.

Maritime Museums online collections database. It currently contains a large range of

Power and Bias Issues
Data analysis is inherently biased as a result of the user, and we encounter bias in the data
in several different steps of the analytical process. Access to the original collection at the
Wisconsin Maritime Museum is a key issue of power, one that is addressed by the
digitization of the Carus photographs, and more broadly, the digital collection as a whole.
Digitization does not mean the complete preservation of collected materials, and is not
without its own problems of access and bias. Much like issues of bias in digital workflows,
digital collections and digitization faces the same hurdles as any online resourcean
expectation for preexisting end-user knowledge, simple hardware requirements, and a
common language. Furthermore, database classification schemes can present issues of
bias. How the collection data is classified reflects a certain perspective of the organizer,
which then impacts how the data is received and understood by users (Thornley, Bustillo,
& Supprian, 2022). In the case of the Wisconsin Martime Museum online database, this is
reflected in the language used in the description categories. It assumes the researcher is
familiar with maritime terminology and the geography of the region, which could present
problems for researchers working with data frame categories or searching for specific
information they may not know how to describe. An example of this issue using the MoMA
dataset can be seen below: if a researcher was attempting to understand medium
frequencies and was not familiar with art terminology, they could find it difficult to search
for a specific medium as industry terminology such as gouache, chromogenic color
print, or lithograph are not commonly understood descriptive terms.
Fig.4: A value count created using a Pandas dataframe, listing the most
common mediums in the MoMA Collection repository
This can also be an issue for researchers with a first language other than Englishthese
datasets, and coding in general, rely on the idea that English is the lingua franca of
research. Another potential issue of power is financial. Funding constraints for heritage
materials is recognized as a global issue for digitization projects (Kumary & Pandey, 2020).
Digitization, and any project dealing with digital data, requires a fair amount of financial
overhead that many small cultural institutions simply cannot afford. Data analytics for
digital collections necessitates personnel who are familiar with the workflows and
understand the unique issues of the institution, or paid training for current staff. Digital
projects also require ongoing funding to maintain servers and project hosting.
Finally, we of course find bias in the original data (the media in the online collection) itself.
In the case of the Carus Collection, we as researchers are limited by the photographs Cpt.
Carus took and the information he chose to provide about them. As Cpt. Carus is
deceased, any information we glean from the media is based on our own inferences about
his work rather than from the primary source.
Power Mitigation
Funding and financials
In an ideal world, there would be a plethora of grants and funding for cultural institutions
such as the Maritime Museum. While funding can be difficult to come by, data
visualizations can assist in mitigating financial power issues. Simple collections analytics
can be used as an effective argumentative or persuasive tool for increased funding,
showcasing the breadth of existing collections to shareholders and community members,
and assisting educational outreach programs. These analytics and visualizations are
commonly used within GLAM institutions to make improvements to budget planning and
service assessments (Finch & Flenner, 2016).
Data analysis and further mitigation
While issues such as language or terminology are not easily mitigated, regular collection
and usage evaluation can highlight points for improvement by curators and archivists. Data
analysis for collections is an integral part of the digitization life cycle. By performing routine
collection evaluations, as described above, researchers and collections managers are
able to both monitor the condition of the database and continue to assess patron needs
(Baucom, 2019).
One of the most important ways we can mitigate power with this technology proposal is
through deeper understanding of the original datas context. Pedersen (2022) illustrates
how Python workflows can be combined with MARC to perform large-scale data analysis
on collections to undertake a diversity audit, something which allows both curators and
researchers to understand gaps in the original collection. This could be applied to the
original Carus collection to better comprehend potential bias based on location or
demography within the original data. Comparative analysis with similar collections in
different cultural heritage institutions may also be an effective method of power mitigation.
Sinn (2013) utilizes mapping and data analysis to highlight use contexts in digital
collections, showcasing how researchers interact with and understand the media they are
studying. If applied to the WMM digital collections, this kind of workflow could highlight
potential gaps in their collection when compared to usages of other similar institutions.
It is important to note that these technologies are not only limited to assisting GLAM
professionals and researchers. Pandas, and data analysis workflows in general, showcase
how basic data analytics can be used as a launching pad for further digital humanities
research, provide potential data for marketing and grant funding, and encourage public
interest in cultural heritage sites. Ultimately, the Wisconsin Maritime Museum, while a
small institution, has taken on what is often a costly and time-consuming process in order
to create a more accessible heritage collection. By introducing some small potential
changes to its established database, the museum could generate exciting new avenues of
engagement for researchers who otherwise would not have utilized their fascinating
collection of maritime history.
